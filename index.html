June 6th 1944 D-Day....

156,000 American, British, and Canadian forces embarked on the largest amphibious military assault in history, to liberate Nazi occupied France.

D-Day is widely refered to as the beginning of the end of the war in Europe. Within 11 months of the invasion Germany would surrender unconditionally, marking the end of Nazi tyranny in Europe.

From June 6th to August 21st the Allies landed more than 2 million men on the established beach head. They suffered extremely high casualites with 72,911 killed, and 153,475 wounded. German losses included over 240,000 casualties and 200,000 captured.

9,387 Americans are still interred near the beaches, at the Normandy American Cemetary and Memorial granted in perpetuity by France to the United States.

This story follows the 502nd Parachute Infantry Regiment of the 101st Airborne Division from June 6th to June 13th 1944...

Methodology:


Unit disposition and movement data for much of World War 2 is not accessible in a GIS format, requiring georeferencing reference materials built on a cartesian coordinate system to a geographic coordinate system.


Once the maps were georeferenced, troop locations and movements were digitized as accurately as possible, but still genericized due to the inaccuracies of after action reports and maps.


Troop movement animations were performed using D3 to import the geoJson digitized troop paths, generate a linear path of movement and animate the marker movement along the path.


Purpose:


I became very interested in this topic listening to the stories my Grandfather would tell me. He was a member of the 101st Airborne during World War II as a replacement Mortar man after the Battle of the Bulge. Much of his training and friendships during the war came from experienced airborne troopers who had survived Normandy, Holland, and Bastogne. The reverence he had for these men's stories left a huge impact on me as a young child and fostered my interest in this part of history into adulthood. I wanted this project to focus on his regiment (502nd) to gain a better understanding of the men he looked up to and what they went through, and continue their stories now that he is no longer able to tell them in his advanced stage of alzheimers...
